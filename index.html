1

Choosing your system size

Figuring out the correct system size can be one of the trickiest and most overlooked pieces of the puzzle when deciding on your solar panel system. Most solar installers will recommend your system size simply by analyzing your past 12 months energy usage. Although this is accurate for most, there are a couple of reasons it may not work for everyone:

#1: Your maximum savings are not at the top of the installers priority list

Cost per watt is the name of the game. As long as that cost per watt on your new system is less than what you are currently paying for electricity through your utility provider you are as good as sold in the eyes of most installers. Because of this you may end up installing a system that produces more than what you actually need based on their recommendations. You may think you win because you are paying less than your old bill, but they actually win because they made more money off of your deal. When maximizing your energy savings it is very important that the solar offset is not too far off from your actual intended usage.

#2: Past use may not be the best indication of future use

When choosing your system size it is important to keep in mind that your current usage right now over the past year can (and probably will) evolve throughout time. When installing your panels it is important to plan for future changes so that your selected system will not become inadequate should your energy needs go up. Having to add on additional panels can be an expensive venture that can significantly eat into the savings you realized when switching to solar to begin with. Below is a list of the most common things to plan for that will effect your energy use:

Purchasing an Electric Vehicle

Home Office / Remote-Work

Family Size Changes

Home Add-ons (Additional Rooms)

Installing Pool / Jacuzzi

LOWER MONTHLY PAYMENTS DON'T ALWAYS MEAN LOWER COST

2

Choosing how to finance

Figuring out the best way to pay for your system is perhaps the most important decision that effects how much you will actually save when switching to solar. If you can afford to pay for your panels is cash and have no need to finance you will be able to secure the best deal on your system - hands down. However with systems costs ranging from $15,000 - $40,000 on average this is simply not an option for most people.

Comparing 10 year vs. 20 year solar loan options

Many solar advertisements lure you in with the promise of low interest / low monthly-payment loans that require no money down and will beat your electricity bill and save you money. While most of their claims are true, in many cases the loans they are offering may not be the best value for your money.


Remember, as stated in section 1: the name of the game is cost per watt. As long as the solar company is beating your electric bill their offer will seem like it's an amazing deal. However when you get down into the details you will see that not all solar loans are created equal.

Higher dealer fees + longer loan = you pay more in total

You will notice that 20 year loans will leave you with the lowest monthly payments, and offer equally attractive low interest rates just like 10 year loans. However, you will be paying this loan for double the length of time and thus paying a higher amount of interest on the loan in total.


Additionally there will be extremely high "dealer fees" or "loan origination fees" that are included into your loan when you choose a 20 year option. These fees can range from 5-20% of your total loan. The reason many people do not notice them is that they are only looking at their monthly payment, which will be cheaper than the 10 year option. 


Introducing: The HELOC...a 3rd, and potentially better loan option

"HOME EQUITY LINE OF CREDIT"

Most people have not thought of, or heard of using a HELOC for financing their solar install. This is because almost all solar companies will not tell you about this option as they make money off of selling you the solar loan. HELOCs can offer a better rate than a traditional solar loan with less fees, and are typically easy to get approved for (if you have equity in your home). 


Additionally, a lot of people skip over the HELOC option because they do not want their home tied to another loan (HELOC loans are backed by the equity in your home). However, what most people do not know is solar loans work the same way and are also backed by your homes value.


If you are able to get approved for a HELOC in an amount that will cover your solar install you may be looking at a better interest rate, and overall less total cost on your system for maximum savings.
